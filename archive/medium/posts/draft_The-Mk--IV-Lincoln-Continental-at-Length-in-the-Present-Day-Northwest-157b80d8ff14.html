The Mk. IV Lincoln Continental at Length in the Present-Day Northwest

A good drive in an especially raucous or communicative motorcar over a challenging bit of road (technically or otherwise) can leave the enthusiast “wrung out” — which is to say squeezed free of juice or twisted dry, naturally (presumably of satisfaction) as well as the vehicle “wrung out,” in turn, implying that its limits were aggressively and repeatedly exceeded to a sufficient degree to support a comprehensive review of its operator relationship. Both I and my 1976 Lincoln Continental are thoroughly wrung out with each other at the moment after traversing 500 NorthWestern miles yesterday — which briefly included Tacoma’s industrially superwide, but imminently gentrified streets, Seattle’s near-vertical hill starts, and the world-class congestion on U.S. Interstate 5 plus the subsequent Apple Maps-preferred detours through smaller satellite communities on two-lane State roads. I’ve just gotten back, meaning there’ll never be a better time to discuss the particular nuances of its driving experience.
