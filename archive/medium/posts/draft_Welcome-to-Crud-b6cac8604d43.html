Yes, automotive media does need us.

Since I could be found reading Autoweek instead of paying attention in high school classes, I have thought and ranted a lot about the core functions of car-themed publication. I believe the word journalism in “auto journalism” should include some “watchdog” purpose: diligent critical reporting on the industry behind the automotive products we use. Historically, car publications have left much to be desired in this regard, shunting the hard news reporting to traditional news sources and — most recently — to tech publications like Wired, The Verge, and Gizmodo, who now all employ full-time reporters on the automotive beat.

There has never been a ProPublica for the automotive industry, nor would I envy the folks who’d endeavor to create one whatsoever, but I believe the cozy relationships auto journalists/publications maintain with manufacturers’ PR departments leave a convenient opportunity for an independent, less-than-connected media company to maintain a unique cheque on both the industry and those who cover it.

From “CrossCabriolet: The Ego Divide:”

At the turn of the century, though, it was not unusual to go a day without accessing the internet. Today, people are still buying the few flops the industry has left to offer — making what is most likely the second-largest purchase of their life’s current epoch without consulting the volumes of diverse, intelligent, and articulate opining now accessible instantly free of charge via the subsidized slates that lightly jostle in their jean pockets as they wiggle their signatures on dealer paperwork.
