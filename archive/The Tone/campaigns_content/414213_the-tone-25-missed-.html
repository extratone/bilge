GEORGE ORWELL'S FIRST TIME

Another [unit of time] gone in our tyrannical dystopia!
The Movie Closet tackled those Campy Halloween films, as the Day of Darkness approaches. Funny enough, I've just read George Orwell's first book-length work, called Down and Out in Paris and London, in just under two days because I've been living in my car in Portland, and Hawthorn found it fitting to lend it to me. In two parts, it depicts Orwell's ~five-year-long poverty in 1930s Paris and London, respectively, and does so without pretentiousness, which was refreshing and surprising. (Embarrassingly, it took a state of pseudohomelessness to get me to read a volume this quickly.)

“In practice nobody cares whether work is useful or useless, productive or parasitic; the sole thing demanded is that it shall be profitable.”

Generally, Orwell's argument is that poverty must be thoroughly experienced to be understood; even, that it should be by just about everyone, and I agree.
